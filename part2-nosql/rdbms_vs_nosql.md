## Database Recommendation
For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, I would recommend MySQL as the primary database. Healthcare systems deal with sensitive and critical data such as patient details, appointments, prescriptions, billing, and treatment history. In such cases, accuracy and reliability matter more than flexibility. MySQL follows the ACID model, which means transactions are atomic, consistent, isolated, and durable. This is very important when multiple operations must succeed together, such as updating a patient record, generating a bill, and recording a payment. A failure in the middle should not leave the system in a broken state.
MongoDB, on the other hand, is more aligned with BASE principles, where the system focuses more on availability and eventual consistency. That can be useful for flexible or rapidly changing data, but for the core patient management system, eventual consistency may introduce risks. In healthcare, even a small inconsistency can create operational or legal problems. From a CAP theorem perspective, the patient system should lean more toward consistency than availability during conflict situations.
If the startup also wants to add a fraud detection module, then my answer changes slightly. I would still keep MySQL for the main patient and billing system, but I would consider adding MongoDB or another analytics-friendly datastore for fraud detection. Fraud detection usually involves large volumes of logs, events, patterns, and semi-structured data. So the best design would be MySQL for the core system of record, with MongoDB supporting fraud analytics separately.
